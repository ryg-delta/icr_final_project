To what extent does the neglect of women's health within healthcare stem from barriers in intercultural communication, and how can addressing these barriers lead to more equitable healthcare outcomes for women?

Neglect of women's health stems from a combination of biases, which hinder trust and communication between women and healthcare providers. Addressing these biases holistically through targeted strategies such as intercultural praxis can promote trust and equitable healthcare outcomes.

Cultural Biases

“It must be all in your head. It can’t possibly hurt that much.”

Cultural biases impact how symptoms and conditions are perceived and communicated between providers and patients. Historical views on gender have shown women as “overemotional” and “dramatic” while men are expected to be stoic when expressing pain. These persistent biases make women’s concerns seem less credible, contributing to delays in diagnosis and dismissals of health concerns Circle Text

Healthcare systems and providers have been trained and made aware of gender biases, so the persistent health inequities could be attributed to more prominent biases.

Systemic Biases

“I’m just going to prescribe you birth control for your endometriosis.”

The healthcare system often overlooks women’s health in medical training, mostly focusing on health issues through a lens built on hegemony. This gap in education perpetuates biases among providers, resulting in a lack of knowledge and more importantly awareness in women specific health issues.
Focusing on systemic reform may divert crucial attention from more feasible changes on the institutional level or provider level. There are also other health concerns that affect both men and women that must be considered.
Green Circle Text

Institutional Biases

“The exact etiology of endometriosis remains unclear and is not yet fully understood within the current body of medical research.”

Historically, medical research has neglected and underfunded studies on female-specific conditions, slowing down advancements in diagnosis and treatment for issues like endometriosis and idiopathic pelvic pain. This neglect has further led to distrust among women regarding their healthcare issues, leading to noncompliance.
While institutional bias in healthcare is a well known barrier to communication, the inherent complexity of women's health conditions plays a more substantial role in influencing health outcomes. Women's conditions are often multifaceted and difficicult to diagnose, leading to significant delays in diagnoses. This is further exacerbated by patients not complying with provider's orders.

A Framework Built on Listening.

To address the shortcomings of women’s health, which stems from several biases and male privilege, a holistic framework which integrates all six principles of intercultural praxis along all levels of communication. This framework will consider Hofstede’s cultural dimension of masculinity/femininity to identify and challenge the cultural, structural and institutional biases that exist within healthcare systems. By applying this framework, it can better improve trust and promote equitable outcomes for women.
Improvements in Meso-Level Communication: Rectify Systemic and Organizational Practices Framing: Acknowledge how the current healthcare system operates within a cultural context that has historically undervalued women’s health. Positioning: Identify the power dynamics between patients and providers, which stem from hegemonic beliefs in the system. Thus, by adopting an intersectional lens, healthcare systems can identify and dismantle communication barriers which affect women Action: Include trainings based off the lived experiences of women in medical education to help providers understand how women’s concerns are affected in the healthcare system
