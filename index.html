To what extent does the neglect of women's health within healthcare stem from barriers in intercultural communication, and how can addressing these barriers lead to more equitable healthcare outcomes for women?

Neglect of women's health stems from a combination of biases, which hinder trust and communication between women and healthcare providers. Addressing these biases holistically through targeted strategies such as intercultural praxis can promote trust and equitable healthcare outcomes.

Gender Biases

“It must be all in your head. It can’t possibly hurt that much.”

Gender biases impact how symptoms and conditions are perceived and communicated between providers and patients. Gender norms have characterized women as “overemotional” and “dramatic” while men are expected to be stoic when expressing pain. These persistent biases make women’s concerns seem less credible, contributing to delays in diagnosis and dismissals of health concerns.
Gender bias in healthcare arises from historical norms and pervasive cultural stereotypes contributing to the [silenced history] of women’s health. Conditions like endometriosis were historically misdiagnosed as hysteria, reflecting male-dominated medical practices (Jones, 2015)​. Women, especially women of color, face compounded barriers due to [intersectionality], where both gendered and racial biases undermine their concerns (Grace & MacBride-Stewart, 2007)​.
While multiple biases exist, gender bias is often the most significant driver of inequities in healthcare. Historical stereotypes of women being "overly emotional" or "hysterical" heavily influence diagnostic delays and treatment decisions. For example, endometriosis is often dismissed as psychosomatic, reflecting gender norms rather than systemic issues (Jones, 2015; Samulowitz et al., 2018)​

Systemic Biases

“I’m just going to prescribe you birth control for your endometriosis.”

The healthcare system often overlooks women’s health in medical training, mostly focusing on health issues through a lens built on male hegemony. This gap in education perpetuates biases among providers, resulting in a lack of knowledge and more importantly, a lack of awareness in women specific health issues.
Systemic biases in medical education perpetuate gender inequities by prioritizing men, leaving professionals unprepared to address women’s health concerns (Patrick-Smith & Bull, 2024; Vela et al., 2022)​​. The power dynamic between men and women often places men in positions of authority, both in medical education and practice, reinforcing a structure based on male [privilege] that dismisses women’s lived experiences (Jones, 2015; Tuana, 2006)​​. Addressing these systemic issues through equitable training, awareness of power imbalances, and inclusive curricula is essential to dismantle barriers to trust and effective care (Vela et al., 2022)
Systemic biases in medical education and training perpetuate knowledge gaps among providers, providing a greater impact on women's health outcomes. Addressing systemic education gaps might yield more immediate improvements than tackling broader cultural or institutional biases (Patrick-Smith & Bull, 2024; Vela et al., 2022)

Institutional Biases

“The exact etiology of endometriosis remains unclear and is not yet fully understood within the current body of medical research.”

Historically, medical research has neglected and underfunded studies on female-specific conditions, slowing down advancements in diagnosis and treatment for issues like endometriosis. Such neglect has further led to distrust among women regarding their healthcare issues, leading to noncompliance. It is a clear example of [silenced history].
Chronic underfunding of women’s health research delays advancements in diagnosing and treating female-specific conditions like endometriosis​. This neglect is rooted in [privilege], as research priorities have focused on male health concerns, marginalizing women’s needs (Seear, 2009b)​​. Policies shaped by cultural hegemony further reinforce inequities by failing to address diverse perspectives in healthcare systems (Jones, 2015; Tuana, 2006)​​. Such ignorance shows how women's concerns continue to be ignored, and knowledge about endometriosis is left undone.
Institutional neglect, rather than individual gender bias or systemic bias, is the core driver of disparities in women's healthcare as trickles down to medical education and treatment. Without significant changes to these funding mechanisms, cultural training alone cannot resolve mistrust (Hudson, 2022; Seear, 2009b)​

A Framework Built on Listening.

To address the shortcomings of women’s health, which stems from several biases and male privilege, a framework that incorporates all three prominent biases is crucial. Each bias influences each other, making them interwoven, as such, the solution must also interweave all three biases to properly break down barriers in communication. This framework will consider Hofstede’s cultural dimension of masculinity/femininity as well as Sorrell's intercultural praxis to identify and challenge the biases that exist within healthcare systems. By applying this framework, it can better improve trust and promote equitable outcomes for women.

Improvements in Micro-Level Communication: Patient-Provider Interactions

  • Inquiry: Encourage providers to approach patients with openness, recognizing their lived experiences, especially those from marginalized backgrounds.
  • Dialogue: Create a safe and trusting environment for women to voice their concerns, without being dismissed.
  • Reflection: Providers should be aware of their own biases and assumptions about women’s health, focusing on their own positionality within a male-centric healthcare system.

Improvements in Meso-Level Communication: Organizational and Systemic Practices

  • Framing: Acknowledge how the current healthcare system operates within a cultural context that has historically undervalued women’s health.
  • Positioning: Identify the power dynamics between patients and providers, which stem from hegemonic beliefs in the system.
  • Action: Include trainings based on the lived experiences of women in medical education to help providers understand how women’s concerns are considered in the healthcare system.

Improvements in Macro-Level Communication: Institutional Awareness

  • Positioning: Identify the positions of power in healtchare and their potential influences on research and healthcare.
  • Dialogue: Raise awareness about women’s health so that more research funding can go toward uncovering better diagnostic methods and treatments.
  • Action: Recognize that trust between patients and providers is often thin, and allow for alternate treatments and procedures alongside Western medicine
